# THE MOON RECORDS — SONIC BRIEF
## Positive Definition

This document defines what a Moon Records release sounds like. It is the counterpart to `not-the-moon-records.md`. Both documents are equally binding.

---

## THE FUNDAMENTAL POSITION

The Moon Records is beatless. Entirely and without exception.

This is not a restriction — it is a definition. The music here is not ambient-adjacent, not mostly-beatless, not "ambient passages in an otherwise rhythmic work." It is music that has made a specific decision: to exist outside tempo, outside pulse, outside the architecture of rhythmic production. That decision must be total.

What is permitted within that space is wide. Lo-fi warmth at one end of the spectrum. Deep drone and pure tone at the other. Between them: texture, field recording, synthesis, decomposition, accumulation — all of it, as long as it is beatless.

---

## THE SPECTRUM

The Moon Records occupies a specific range:

**Lo-Fi Warm → Soundscape → Drone → Deep Drone**

These are not separate categories — they are positions on a single spectrum. A release can inhabit one position or move across several. The spectrum exists so that A&R decisions have a shared reference frame.

### Lo-Fi Warm

The warm end of the spectrum. Characteristics:

- Tape saturation, slight speed instability, organic analog texture
- Room presence — you can feel the space the recording happened in
- Melodic elements are permitted here: slow, minimal, often piano or guitar, often heavily processed
- Warmth is earned through specific analog decisions, not through plugin presets
- Reference register: Harold Budd, Grouper, Stars of the Lid (at their most intimate), William Basinski's early loop works

**The distinction:** lo-fi warmth on this label means the warmth of a worn recording medium, a specific room, a specific hour. It does not mean "pleasant." It means specific.

### Soundscape

The middle of the spectrum. Characteristics:

- Field recordings as structural elements, not texture
- The relationship between the recorded world and synthesis — how they inhabit the same space
- Environments made audible: industrial, natural, architectural, urban at night
- The composition is in the editing and layering, not the performance
- Reference register: Chris Watson, Lawrence English (acoustic), Loscil, The Caretaker (mid-period)

**The distinction:** a soundscape on this label is not a field recording with atmosphere added. It is a compositional act. The field recording is a material, not a source.

### Drone

Sustained tones with internal movement. Characteristics:

- One or a small number of pitches held for extended duration
- The interest is in the internal texture of the sustained tone — its overtones, its movement, its decay and renewal
- Microtonality is welcome — pitches that exist between standard tuning
- Synthesis, bowed instruments, extended vocal technique, organ, processed guitar
- Reference register: Eliane Radigue, Stars of the Lid (orchestral works), Tim Hecker (mid-period), Grouper (drone works)

**The distinction:** a drone is not a pad. A pad is a timbral choice. A drone is a compositional commitment.

### Deep Drone

The far end. Characteristics:

- Minimal pitch movement, maximal timbral depth
- Sub-bass presence with near-silence in the upper register
- Long durations — 20 minutes or more is not unusual and is often correct
- The listener's attention is required to perceive the movement at all
- Reference register: La Monte Young (long tones), Eliane Radigue (Trilogie de la Mort), Pauline Oliveros (deep listening works)

**The distinction:** deep drone is the most demanding form on the label. It asks the listener to slow down to the music's pace, not the reverse.

---

## DURATION

Duration is a compositional element, not a format constraint.

- A 6-minute piece that holds nothing back: correct.
- A 45-minute piece that earns every minute: correct.
- An 8-minute piece that is really 4 minutes with 4 minutes of unnecessary fade: incorrect.

There is no minimum. There is no maximum. The duration of a release should be the exact duration the music requires.

EP format: 2–4 tracks. Album format: whatever the work demands.

---

## SILENCE

Silence is structural.

The absence of sound in a Moon Records release is not empty space — it is a compositional element of equal weight to the sound. A track that knows when to go completely quiet, and holds that quiet for long enough that the listener begins to hear the room, has understood something fundamental.

Silence is never used as a fade-out shortcut. It is used as a statement.

---

## TEXTURE AND SURFACE

The audio surface of a Moon Records release should carry material quality. Like the visual system demands grain on every surface, the audio demands texture. Clean digital synthesis without surface texture is a system violation — not because analog is superior, but because smoothness without intention signals a decision that was not made.

**Material qualities that are correct:**
- Tape hiss at the threshold of audibility
- Room tone present and acknowledged
- Slight pitch instability on long tones (analog characteristic)
- Vinyl surface noise where it is integral to the work, not decorative

**The distinction:** texture is not lo-fi as aesthetic signaling. Crackle added to a cleanly recorded piece to imply age is not texture — it is costume. Texture is the actual grain of how the recording was made and what it was made with.

---

## SPACE AND MIX

- **Depth before width.** The stereo field is secondary to the sense of depth — near and far, present and distant.
- **Reverb as environment, not effect.** If reverb is present, it should define the space the music inhabits. Not decorative. Not "ambient treatment."
- **Sub-bass:** present or absent by compositional decision. Not habitually present. When it appears, it matters.
- **Dynamic range:** preserved. The quietest and loudest moments of a Moon Records release should be far apart. Compressed ambient is a contradiction.
- **Mastering:** loud masters are incorrect. This music should not compete for loudness. The listener should turn it up.

---

## THE CORE QUESTION

After one complete listen — which for deep drone may be 40 minutes:

> *Does this music know what it is doing?*

Not: is it pleasant. Not: does it fit a reference. The question is whether every element — the duration, the texture, the silence, the movement — exists because a specific decision was made. Not by default. Not because that's how ambient is made. Because this particular work required it.

If yes: the rest of this document is secondary.

If no: more texture and more reverb will not fix it.

---

## A NOTE ON MELODY

Melody is not forbidden. The lo-fi warm end of the spectrum can carry significant melodic content. The question is whether the melody is structural — whether the work requires it — or whether it is borrowed from a different register of music and placed into an ambient frame.

Melodic content that implies resolution, that builds and releases in the manner of song structure, belongs to a different kind of music. A Moon Records melody is a drone that moves.
